Taxi Chaos

Taxi Chaos is a 3D driving game inspired by classic arcade racers — a blend of chaos, precision, and time pressure. The goal was to simulate a living city where the player balances speed with control, picking up NPC passengers and dropping them at destinations before the timer runs out. What started as a college project became a full experiment in modular systems, AI traffic behavior, and performance optimization in Unity.

Development Journey

The Idea

I’ve always loved how arcade driving games capture pure adrenaline with minimal mechanics — just you, the car, and a ticking clock. The concept for Taxi Chaos started as a fun twist: combine Crazy Taxi’s urgency with simulation-like systems. The plan was to create an explorable low-poly city where AI cars, pedestrians, and mission systems all co-exist — something both playful and technical.

How I Planned It

The core pillars I defined early were:

  • Dynamic Vehicle Physics — cars that feel responsive and believable but still arcade-like.
  • AI Traffic & Pathing — self-driven vehicles navigating different routes through the city grid.
  • Passenger Missions — a loop where pickup, delivery, and scoring systems link seamlessly.
  • Reward Loop — money earned from speed and safety, reinforcing skill-based play.

I structured my Trello board around these pillars, breaking them into small, testable modules. That approach kept development organized and let me test features independently before integration.

How I Completed It

The first major system was car control. I rebuilt it from scratch using a modular script structure: GetInput() for steering, HandleMotor() for torque, HandleSteering() for angle adjustment, and ApplyAntiRoll() for suspension stability. After countless test drives and physics tuning, the car felt right — quick, weighty, and responsive at higher speeds.

The AI traffic system came next. Instead of using NavMesh agents (which proved heavy for large maps), I built a lightweight waypoint-based controller. Each car follows its path independently, obeys stop signals, and reacts to nearby vehicles. The system’s simplicity allowed hundreds of AI cars to run smoothly even on mid-tier hardware.

For the passenger system, I created a modular mission manager — randomizing pickup spots, generating nearby destinations, and attaching dialogue snippets for personality. Passengers trigger events like timers, cash rewards, and sound effects, creating a satisfying gameplay rhythm.

By the end of development, I had an entire gameplay loop functional: drive → pick up → deliver → earn → repeat. Seeing the systems interact in real time — traffic reacting to collisions, passengers commenting, and UI updating dynamically — was one of the most rewarding parts of building Taxi Chaos.

What Needs Work

While the core systems work, there’s still plenty of room to grow. The current car physics are fun but lack subtle handling at higher speeds. The AI sometimes collides during intersections, and mission variety is limited to basic pickup-and-drop loops.

  • Implement lane-switching and overtaking for AI traffic.
  • Refine drift and steering curves for smoother control.
  • Add random passenger events — e.g., impatient customers or bonus fares.
  • Improve visual feedback with speed blur and tire smoke effects.

Future Updates

The long-term goal for Taxi Chaos is to evolve it into a full arcade driving experience. Planned updates include:

  • Adding a mini-map system with GPS-style routing.
  • Introducing day-night cycles and dynamic weather.
  • Expanding cities and mission types for better replay value.
  • Integrating mobile touch controls and releasing it on Android as a casual driving game.

Every iteration brings it closer to the original vision — a fast, reactive, living city where chaos meets control.
