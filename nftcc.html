Nightmares of the Creepy Cube

Nightmares of the Creepy Cube is a 2D stealth-survival game developed in Unity, where you play as a creepy cube escaping through a nightmare world while avoiding hostile robots. I handled all programming, visual effects, and music, focusing on core gameplay, cosmetics, and early level design.

Development Journey

The Idea

It’s funny — most game programmers start out making a simple 2D platformer, but I never did. Nightmares of the Creepy Cube was my first real attempt at one, and it quickly became much more ambitious than expected. I got inspired after seeing Sqube Darkness on mobile — its eerie tone and sharp silhouettes caught my eye. I wanted to recreate that dark, moody vibe but with deeper mechanics: enemies that react to movement, destructible terrain, and a stealth system that rewards patience.

How I Planned It

My main goal was to make it a mobile-first platformer — something I could publish on the Play Store with ads, rewarded videos, and future in-app cosmetic purchases.

I designed two planned modes:

  • Endless Mode, where the terrain generates infinitely and survival time defines the score.
  • Level Progression Mode, a handcrafted journey with 50 levels.

For the core loop, I decided on three pillars: procedural level generation, AI-driven hazards, and dynamic terrain destruction. This meant all the gameplay systems — enemies, explosions, and obstacles — had to talk to the same ground system in real time.

How I Completed It

The foundation was the Ground Generator built using Unity’s Tilemap API. It procedurally creates terrain chunks ahead of the player, adjusting step height, adding bumps, and occasionally spawning spikes or termites. It’s tuned for randomness but with built-in constraints so the player never encounters an impossible jump.

The enemy AI system added life (and chaos) to the world:

  • Termites detect motion, chase, and self-destruct after locking onto the player — their explosions dynamically carve holes in the tilemap via the MakeHole method.
  • Drones sweep across the map with a real-time vision cone mesh, built from procedural raycasts. If they spot a moving player, they fire homing missiles that track and explode, also altering the terrain.

All sounds and music were made from scratch — I wanted complete control over the game’s tone. Even the cosmetics (the player’s “eye” designs) were drawn by me.

At this stage, the game runs fully with PC controls, infinite procedural generation, all enemy types functional, and dynamic destruction integrated into gameplay.

What Needs Work

There’s still a lot to build — the level mode with handcrafted challenges, ads and reward systems, and achievement-based cosmetic unlocks are in progress. The onboarding also needs improvement; players don’t immediately understand that hiding in shadows and staying still prevents drone detection.

Future updates will add more enemy types, better rewards, and mobile touch controls. Once polished, Nightmares of the Creepy Cube will be my first Play Store release — a dark, reactive world built entirely from procedural code.
